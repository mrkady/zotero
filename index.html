In brief
Follow through the prompts in this document in conjunction with the workshop instructions to get acquainted with what Zotero can do to make your research life easier. Objectives

At the end of this workshop, and by extension this document, you will be able to:

  • Summarize the utility of reference management for academic research.
  • Apply Zotero to:
    • Import references from the internet, PDFs, or PubMed.
    • Organize references with collections and tags.
    • Annotate references and take notes.
  • Design a custom RSS feed in PubMed for keeping up to date with new literature.

What is (and why) Zotero?

Zotero [zoh-TAIR-oh] is a free and open-source reference management software, a program which stores references (e.g., journal articles, book chapters) and can generate citations for them. There are many reference management softwares, EndNote and Mendeley being popular alternatives to Zotero. I have experience with and like all of them. They all do much the same things, and references can be moved between them rather easily. The skills learned in this workshop largely apply to any of them.

So why Zotero specifically? Zotero is completely free, with no features locked behind a paywall. That makes it more accessible to you both now and in the future, whatever institutions your career takes you to. It’s also, in my opinion, the best one.

Why use a reference manager?

But why go through the effort of managing references at all? Reference managers are usually thought of as citation engines, but I think that is actually one of their less important features. Effective reference management can alleviate many of the hardships of interacting with the literature:

  • Organizing papers by subject, keywords, read/unread status, etc.
  • Keeping PDFs of papers straight (and giving them decent names)
  • Taking notes, both in annotations to the PDFs themselves or in standalone notes
  • Staying abreast of newly published papers

If you think of these softwares less like citation engines and more like your personal library of literature, the literature becomes less of a daunting slog and more manageable and fun. I developed this workshop with the aim of introducing these other less-appreciated features of reference managers.

Activity #2: Importing and Organizing References

Everyone reading this has likely added a bookmark to their web browser at one point or another. Bookmarks are easy to make, low stakes, and useful; they can be to a website you use every day or to something you swear you’ll get back to later.

Think of adding references to your reference manager like bookmarking papers. Easy, low stakes, and useful. You don’t necessarily need to read each item cover-to-cover to justify adding it. It’s useful if you ever want to come back to it, very useful if you refer to it often, and borderline required if you ever plan on citing it.

References can be added to Zotero in many different ways. The mission for this activity is to import references into Zotero from a variety of sources and organize them in case-dependent ways. Each case has its own quirks, but they all represent common use cases. Below you will find a basic Zotero orientation and some tips which apply to all the cases, followed by the 3 cases themselves. Each table will be assigned one case to start off.

Basic Zotero Orientation

When you first boot up Zotero it will look something like this:

The center pane is where your list of references will appear (once you have some). On the left is the Collections Pane, which shows the collections you create (which are a lot like folders in your file manager), as well as some default special collections (like “My Publications”). On the bottom left is the Tag Selector. Tags are a different way of categorizing references, more akin to keywords, although you can define and set your own. On the right is the Item Pane, which shows specific information about the reference selected.

The Add Item(s) by Identifier button (magic wand at the top left of the center pane) is an easy way to add items without opening the web browser. If we enter, say, a DOI: 10.1016/j.cell.2011.02.013, we will be greeted with a new item and an automatically attached PDF:

The DOI links to a 2011 review in Cell titled Hallmarks of Cancer: The Next Generation. The article is part of Cell Press’s Open Archive, so Zotero was able to fetch the full text PDF along with the rest of the bibliographic information. Selecting the item in the center pane will bring up the editable bibliographic info in the Item Pane to the right. Clicking the little arrow at the left of the item will reveal the attachments. Double-clicking on the item will open the PDF in another tab, right in Zotero!

Items and Attachments

Individual references in Zotero take the form of items. An item can have one of many types, e.g., journal article, book, book section, or preprint. An item can have any number of file attachments. While you can attach any type of file, Zotero is most agile with PDF and EPUB attachments, which can be opened and marked up within the program itself. The column headers of the center pane are customizable to your preference by right-clicking one of the headers. You could include, say, the year of publication or journal title as a column if you wanted to.

Collections and Tags

Zotero has two primary way of organizing items. Collections are a lot like folders on your computer; the only difference is that an item can be in any number of collections at once. This can be useful: you could file a reference under multiple subjects (e.g., “cancer”, “immunology”) as well by type (e.g., “reviews”) or something else (e.g., “to cite for 2025 paper”).

To create a collection, you can click the New Collection… icon at the top left of the Collections Pane to the left. Collections can have sub-collections. To add an item to a collection, you can right-click on it and click Add to Collection or drag the item to the collection in the Collections Pane.

Tags are much like tags anywhere else, short labels which often describe content in the form of keywords. You can click on tags in the Tag Selector in the bottom right to filter your library by those tags. Imports from PubMed and certain journal websites can bring with them keywords automatically. You can also manually add custom tags to an item by navigating to the Tags icon in the Item Pane.

Tips for Case A:

Zotero is good at importing PDFs, and can often acquire their metadata automatically. The simplest way to import PDFs is to drag them from your file manager to the center pane. Alternatively, you can click the Add Attachment icon at the top left of the center pane.

Zotero will usually be able to create a parent item with the appropriate metadata from the PDF automatically, but not always. In this case, the attachment will not have a parent item, i.e., it will be “naked”, without any bibliographic data attached to it. This is perfectly okay, but if you want to add metadata or make the item citable, you will need to get the info from another source (like a website) or create an item with the appropriate data manually (the New Item icon at the top left of the center pane). Dragging the attachment into that new item will pair it to that item.

Tips for Case C:

Zotero imports from web links using a browser plugin called Zotero Connector, which you can install from the Zotero website along with the program itself. Connector uses scripts called translators to extract item metadata from a variety of websites. The translators work very well on the sites where most biomedical research is published, including PubMed and those of Springer-Nature, ScienceDirect, Wiley, and many more.

Zotero Connector works best if you pin it to the toolbar of your browser. In its dormant state, the icon will look like this , but when you navigate to a website pertaining to a reference which can be translated into Zotero, the icon will change depending on the item type, e.g., for a journal article or for a book chapter. Seeing this icon change is a reliable indicator that the item will be imported correctly, and clicking it will add the item to your library.

Zotero will try to add the PDF as well. In my experience, ensuring that you already have access to the article—say, though OpenAthens—before you click the icon is the best way to guarantee the PDF comes along. If that fails, you can download the PDF separately and attach it to the item via the “Add Attachment” option.

Discussion points:
  • Can you think of any websites or otherwise unconventional reference items you may want to add to your library? How does Zotero Connector fare in adding them to Zotero?
  • What happens if you’re on a website with multiple reference items linked?
  • Can you add items to your library from Zotero Connector if Zotero isn’t open?

Overall, Zotero is robust at importing many types of items from many types of sources. While different methods have their own nuances, just do whatever makes you most likely to actually add items. If you like, go ahead and drag all the PDFs from your personal literature collection into the center pane; Zotero can take it.

Activity #3: Annotating and Note-Taking

Saving papers is great, but it pays to read a few from time to time. Taking notes while reading is a simple but effective way of distilling the information in an article into the few key takeaways which matter to you. Those notes then serve as a valuable reminders when you want to refer back to something you’ve read, but don’t remember all the details; it’s like teleporting your mind back to when you had just read it (without actually needing to reread it).

Let’s say you wanted to highlight certain key passages or figures of a review you really like, or cap off each article you read with a one or two-sentence summary of your key takeaway to make it easier to cite later, or collate excerpts from a variety of papers in order to compare their protocols. Zotero has a feature-rich note-taking workflow which allows for all of this and more within the application itself.

The mission for this activity is to annotate PDFs with Zotero’s built in tools and explore crafting notes with different types of content and content from multiple different attachments. What follows is another orientation, this time to annotations and notes, and another case.

Everyone has the same case for this activity.

Zotero Orientation, Part 2

When an attachment is open in Zotero, the document can be viewed in the center pane, with the Sidebar on the left and the Context Pane on the right. Each can show different types of information.

Zotero allows you to add Annotations and mark up PDFs a lot like other PDF viewers. When a PDF is open, the tools for different forms of annotations sit above the PDF in the center pane:

Clicking on the Toggle Sidebar icon at the top left and selecting the Show Annotations options will show you an overview of all annotations in the document.

Where Zotero can exceed the capabilities of other PDF viewers is in the synergy between annotations and Notes. Notes are a special type of item that can exist by themselves (“standalone notes”) or belong to a specific item (“item notes”). To create an item note, select the Notes icon from the context menu on the right and click the + next to “Item Notes” and select Add Item Note:

Notes can contain free-form text as well as content from PDFs such as annotations. With an item note open, any text in the PDF can be selected and added to it by selecting it, right-clicking, and clicking Add to Note. Annotations can be added to notes by selecting them in the PDF or sidebar and clicking Add to Note. Content added to notes in this way will also link back to the content in the PDF itself:

Any content from any PDF in your library can be added to any note, so the range of information you can combine with notes is extremely flexible. An item note may contain a series of annotations just from that item along with your own comments, summarizing the main takeaways. In contrast, a standalone note may synthesize content from many sources in a way that makes it easy to reference back to each individual article.

Activity #4: Custom RSS Feed

Imagine if you were, like me, really focused on one thing, like a species of bacteria called L. reuteri. You may decide that anytime a new paper mentions L. reuteri, you want to know about it. Or that every time your hopefully future-postdoc-lab publishes, you want to be the first to read it. Or that you want to at least read the title of each new review published in Nature Reviews So-and-So, in case it interests you. Or any combination of the above. The “Create RSS” feature of PubMed gives you that power, and can integrate with Zotero itself.

This activity is more self-directed. The goal is to create a custom feed based on a PubMed search to help you stay up to date with newly published literature. There is no story problem for this part—it’s all up to you.

Case for Activity #4

Using the “Create RSS” feature of PubMed search, experiment until you craft a search that feels useful to you.

TipGreen flag criteria:

Raise the green flag when everyone at your table has:

  • an RSS feed imported into Zotero
  • they have read an abstract, opened a link, or added an item to their library from their feed
  • they think the feed could be practically useful to their work

Tips for Activity #4

PubMed has different ways to update you of new search hits. It can send you an email every week, or you can create a custom RSS feed which integrates with another program. RSS is a standardized format for generating web feeds, kind of like a social media feed. You can add RSS feeds to Zotero similarly to adding an item. If the RSS feed is linked to a custom PubMed search you’ve created, Zotero will then contain a periodically updated feed of new PubMed entries which match that search, allowing you to browse the abstracts, access URLs, and even add them to your library without ever leaving Zotero. In my opinion, this close integration between where your literature lives and where it comes from makes browsing rather painless.

Steps for making a custom RSS feed from PubMed

1) Go to https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/

2) Search something. Anything will do. The “Advanced” search option makes searching by a specific journal, author, or the like simpler; however, a generic keyword to start off is fine.

Mount Sinai has a good overview of the Advanced Search features of PubMed.

3) Click “Create RSS” under the search bar:

4) Some options will appear. I recommend changing “number of items displayed” to 100, and then hitting “Create RSS”.

5) Copy the RSS feed link and return to Zotero.

6) On the menu bar, navigate to File > New Library > New Feed > From URL…

7) Paste the URL into the appropriate field. The title will automatically populate. Hit “OK”.

Under “Advanced Options”, I typically change the update frequency to every 24 hours, as the default (every 2 hours) feels excessive, but that’s just my personal preference.

8) Voila! There is now a new category in your Collections Pane, Feeds , which contains your custom feed . Unread feed items appear in bold, and since the feed is brand new, it now contains up to 100 bolded items. Items can be marked as read in a few different ways, and as new search hits arise they will appear at the top of the list in bold. When an item in the feed is selected, the Item Pane lists all the info needed to judge whether an article is worth taking a closer look at, like the abstract, as well as links to the PubMed entry and the DOI.

The URL generated for the RSS feed is permanently tied to that search. To edit the feed, run a new search in PubMed with your edited parameters and generate a new link.

WarningA word of warning: too many hits (click to expand)

If you are anything like me, your intellectual eyes are bigger than your stomach. Several feeds, each spawning dozens of new abstracts a day, is likely to result in fatigue; therefore, part of the art of crafting an effective feed is appreciating the volume of hits you will be receiving. Thankfully, there is an easy way to check this in the redesigned PubMed interface.

In the current version of PubMed, the sidebar shows a histogram of search hits by year. Going back to my L. reuteri example, we can see there are 231 hits from the year 2024:

That means a bit less than 1 abstract a day—a manageable amount. In contrast, “lactobacillus” has 4,350 hits in 2024, while “limosilactobacillus reuteri” AND “bioengineering” has only 4. Experimenting till you hit a balance of topic specificity and hit volume will help make your feed more usable.

What follows is a laundry list of other things Zotero is good for, beyond the scope of this workshop. Feel free to tackle them if you finish the activities early, find the activities uninspiring, or on your own after the workshop. They progress in roughly increasing order of difficulty and esotericsim.

NoteCiting items in Microsoft Word (click to expand)

An obvious and regrettable time-constraint-induced gap in this workshop is the actual citing of references in a word processor like Microsoft Word. Citing in Microsoft Word with Zotero is easy with the plugin; Zotero even has plugins for other apps like LibreOffice and Google Docs and integration with other markdown-based systems like R Markdown or Quarto. The plugins are bundled with Zotero itself, so you don’t have to download anything extra. In the case of Microsoft Word, a new Zotero tab will appear at the end of the ribbon at the top. The documentation can help you from there.

Zotero knows how to follow the citation formats of different journals based on a library of Citation Style Language or .csl files. A few styles are loaded by default; all the rest are in the Zotero Style Repository, which is also accessible from the Zotero settings (in the menu bar, choose Zotero > Preferences/Settings… > Cite > Style Manager > Get Additional Styles…). These styles generally perform “correctly” with common item types that aren’t missing metadata. They will likely be good enough for any manuscript submission before meeting the careful eye of the copy editor. Nevertheless, as a rule I always give my automatically generated bibliographies a skim—you’ll be surprised what kinds of garbled metadata make it though the Zotero translators.

NoteHotkeyed and color-coded custom tags (click to expand)

I have a very small set of highly used manual tags: unread, read, fav, etc. By right-clicking on a tag in the tag selector and choosing Assign Color… you can assign a color and number-key hotkey to that tag. If an item has a colored tag, a corresponding colored circle will be displayed next to the title in the center panel. Application of the tag can be toggled with the assigned hotkey, making both the scanning and switching of colored tags very fast and convenient.

NoteCreating footnotes in PowerPoint with Quick Copy and CSL styles (click to expand)

Sometimes you want to cite something in a presentation. I usually see this accomplished with teeny text in the bottom corner. The variety of citation styles used between and within presentations I have seen gives me the impression that most people are crafting them on-the-fly.

There is a better way. Zotero has a feature called Quick Copy, which allows for copy-and-paste of simple bibliographies like those found in the bottom corner of a PowerPoint slide. Dragging items or selecting them and pressing Ctrl/Cmd+Shift+C will allow you to paste them anywhere else. The citation style used is dictated in the settings (in the menu bar, choose Zotero > Preferences/Settings… > Export > Quick Copy > Item Format). If the publicly available citation style files don’t match your preferred format, consider writing your own. Zotero has a built-in .csl editor, but the CSL Visual Editor available online is extremely user-friendly and feature rich.

If your curious, here is my personal style for Quick Copy, which follows an Author, Publication, Year format. I also have one for Quick Copying identifiers (e.g., PMID, DOI) that I use for sharing references with coauthors within Microsoft Word comments, and one not for Quick Copy but which adds the PMCID to the Nature citation style that I use for NIH grant applications.

A nominal rule for citations in NIH grant applications (in fact, the only rule for citations in NIH grant applications) is inclusion of the PMCID if it exists. With the disclaimer that I have never seen anyone care or get docked for this, it can be accomplished with Zotero. If importing from PubMed or PubMed Central, Zotero will bring in the PM(C)ID into the Extra field. For other items, a Zotero plugin exists to add PM(C)IDs automatically upon import/edit or by right-click. A CSL file can read data from the Extra field and thereby include the PMCID if it is present. That is all my custom CSL does; there is another one available online.
NoteMore granular filtering with Saved Searches (click to expand)

Sometimes I want to just see all my notes, or all my attachments, or all journal articles which lack a DOI. The Advanced Search option, accessed by selecting the magnifying glass next to the search bar, allows for more fine-tuned searching of your library based on one or multiple parameters. If you would like to repeatedly referenced these search criteria, you can save an advanced search as a Saved Search by choosing the Save Search dialog. The saved search will then sit below your collections in the collections pane.

NoteSimulate a folder with all your PDFs with file manager Saved Searches (click to expand)

One thing that I didn’t like about Zotero at first, compared to my precious folder with hundreds of PDFs, was that, because of the way Zotero stores attachments, each file is stored in a separate 8-character subfolder within Zotero’s storage folder on your computer. This makes it a bit more cumbersome to retrieve the PDF to, say, email it to a collaborator.

Thankfully, you can created a “Saved Search” in your file manager as well. While the steps will vary depending on the operating system, try searching for all PDFs within the Zotero storage folder and pinning/saving that search to your favorites. This saved search, which can then be further search, does well to approximate the one-big-folder lifestyle.

NoteLibrary and attachment syncing with a Zotero account (click to expand)

You may have seen a Zotero notification badgering you to sync your data by setting up an account. Setting up a Zotero account online and linking it in Zotero (in the menu bar, choose Zotero > Preferences/Settings… > Sync) will allow syncing of your items with the Zotero Web Library, allowing you to access the items anywhere.

There is a caveat. The storage limit for a free Zotero account (300 MB) is adequate for your notes and their embedded images, but will run out if you try to sync every attachment. Therefore, I recommend unchecking Sync full-text content, Sync attachment files in My Library using Zotero, and Sync attachment files in group libraries using Zotero storage.

If having not only the bibliographic data but the full-text and/or attachment/annotation data synced online or between multiple devices is valuable to you, consider paying for Zotero Storage or storing the attachments outside of Zotero’s storage (e.g., with OneDrive or Google Drive) by means of Linked Files. I used to do that; however, if you only ever interact with the PDFs on one device it isn’t worth it. When the time comes to upgrade computer, moving the library is as easy as copy and paste.

NoteCustom file renaming (click to expand)

You may have noticed that if metadata is successfully retrieved with a PDF, Zotero automatically renames the PDF based on the metadata, by default following an Author - Year - Title.pdf format. This can be changed. In the menu bar, choose Zotero > Preferences/Settings… > General > File Renaming > Customize Filename Format… and follow the syntax laid out in the documentation.

If you’re curious, my personal format is:

{{firstCreator suffix = ", "}} 
{{if journalAbbreviation == ""}} 
{{publicationTitle suffix = ", "}} 
{{else}} 
{{journalAbbreviation suffix = ", "}} 
{{endif}} 
{{if itemType == "preprint"}} 
{{repository suffix = ", "}} 
{{endif}} 
{{year suffix = ". "}} 
{{if shortTitle == ""}} 
{{title truncate = "50"}} 
{{else}} 
{{shortTitle truncate = "50"}} 
{{endif}}

which results in an Author, Publication, Year. Title.pdf format.

NoteFinding related papers with ResearchRabbit or Connected Papers (click to expand)

Sometimes when I’m trying to familiarize myself with a new large body of work, I feel like I am reading around a topic while missing important elements of it, or I want to know if any important follow-up work has been done since the articles I’m reading were published. Usually Google Scholar does the trick, but sometimes I’m too clueless to know what to search. There are a few apps which, when given a paper or papers as input, will generate network graphs of relatedness based on citations or keywords. This can help you fill in gaps in your library.

The one that I like the most is ResearchRabbit, which is free. You can create manual libraries or sync a library from Zotero of up to 100 items. ResearchRabbit will then map out Similar Work, Earlier Work, and Later Work, as well as connections between authors. There are lots of interesting features worth exploring.

There are a few apps that do similar things. Connected Papers is one, but the free version limits the number of graphs per month.
